\section{Line Identification}

As a first step in the reverberation mapping analysis, emission lines are identified in the mean spectrum derived from the HST/STIS dataset. This is done for the optical–NIR range $3.900\,\AA$–$9.000\,\AA$  and for the UV range $1.100\,\AA$– $1.700\,\AA$.\\ 
In the optical–NIR range (see Figure \ref{fig:mean_rms_SPECTRUM}), several prominent broad emission lines are identified: Balmer lines from H$\alpha$ to H$\epsilon$; He\,\textsc{i}$\,\lambda\,4471$, He\,\textsc{i}$\,\lambda\,5016$, He\,\textsc{i}$\,\lambda\,5876$, He\,\textsc{i}$\,\lambda\,7065$ and He\,\textsc{ii}$\,\lambda\,4686$; and the low-ionization lines O\,\textsc{i}$\,\lambda\,8446$ and the Ca\,\textsc{ii}$\,\lambda\,8498$ $\lambda\,8542$\,$\lambda\,8662$ triplet. In the UV spectrum (see Figure \ref{fig:UV_uncalibrated_mean_rms}), five broad emission line complexes are identified: Ly$\alpha$, the N\,\textsc{v} $\lambda\lambda1238,\,1242$ doublet; Si\,\textsc{iv} $\lambda\lambda1393,\,1402$; C\,\textsc{iv} $\lambda\lambda1548,\,1550$; and He\,\textsc{ii}$\,\lambda1640$. These lines show noticeable profiles in the rms spectrum and therefore exhibit variable components. While earlier reverberation mapping studies of NGC\,4593 primarily focused on H$\alpha$ and H$\beta$ (e.g. \cite{kollatschny1997balmer, denney2006ngc4593}), the wavelength coverage of the HST/STIS dataset enables the inclusion of the higher order Balmer lines H$\gamma$ and H$\delta$, as well as the He\,\textsc{ii}$\,\lambda\,4686$ and He\,\textsc{i}$\,\lambda\,5876$ line and the low-ionization line O\,\textsc{i}$\,\lambda\,8446$. Since parts of the UV regime is included in the HST/STIS dataset as well, it is possible to monitor Ly$\alpha$, N\,\textsc{v} $\lambda\lambda1238,\,1242$, Si\,\textsc{iv} $\lambda\lambda1393,\,1402$, C\,\textsc{iv} $\lambda\lambda1548,\,1550$, and He\,\textsc{ii}$\,\lambda1640$ for the first time in a reverberation mapping analysis of NGC\,4593.\\
Several narrow forbidden emission lines can be identified in the mean spectrum, with [O\,\textsc{iii}] $\lambda4363$, $\lambda4959$, and $\lambda5007$ being the most prominent. Since they are assumed to be constant over the timescale of the campaign, they show no significant variability in the rms spectrum. The same applies to the two prominent Fe\,\textsc{ii} emission line bands between $\sim 4.500\,\AA$ -- $4.600\,\AA$ and between $\sim 5.200\,\AA$ -- $5.300\,\AA$. Finally, the UV range of the spectrum exhibits semi-forbidden emission lines with the O\,\textsc{iv}]\,$\lambda\lambda\, 1397,\,1399$ doublet, which is blended with the Si\,\textsc{iv} $\lambda\lambda1393,\,1402$ doublet, the N\,\textsc{iv}]\,$\lambda1486$ line and the O\,\textsc{iii}]\,$\lambda\lambda\, 1660,\,1666$ doublet.

\section{Emission-Line and Continua Measurement}

To measure the variability of the identified broad emission lines, light curves are extracted by measuring their fluxes for each epoch. This is performed with a Python-based tool called GECHO, developed by M.\,Probst. This tool can import full campaigns, determine mean and rms spectra, extract light curves and conduct further measurements, such as line-width measurements and lags measurement based on the methods of \cite{gaskell_peterson1986} and \cite{peterson2004}, which are further discussed in Sections \ref{sec:line_profiles} and \ref{sec:time_lag_bh_mass}.\\
The emission line light curve extraction follows the same principle as previous reverberation mapping studies (e.g. \cite{kollatschny1997balmer, probst2025emissionlinecontinuumreverberationmapping}). Figure \ref{fig:gecho_example_line} displays the GECHO graphical user interface (GUI), which provides a side-by-side view of the campaign’s mean and rms spectra. The line flux at each epoch is obtained by integrating the flux density over the wavelength range, marked in red in Figure \ref{fig:gecho_example_line}. The integration boundaries are chosen to include the variable part of the emission line while excluding contributions from neighboring lines. To account for the surrounding continuum, a linear pseudo-continuum is interpolated from two line-free wavelength windows on the blue and red side of the emission line, shown in grey in Figure \ref{fig:gecho_example_line}. The integration limits and pseudo-continua for each line are listed in Table \ref{tab:emission_lines} and the measured integrated line fluxes in the Tables \ref{tab:balmer}, \ref{tab:helium}, \ref{tab:lyo} and \ref{tab:uv}.

\section{Light Curves}
\label{sec:lightcurves}

The wavelength coverage of the HST/STIS dataset enables to measure several continuum light curves in the UV-, optical- and NIR regime. In addition, the photometric light curve of the \textit{Swift} UVW2 band is included in the analysis, presented by \cite{mchardy2018x}. \cite{cackett2018accretion} used this light curve to obtain a UV/optical lag spectrum of the continuum emission relative to the \textit{Swift} UVW2 band. It exhibits a denser sampling than the HST/STIS light curves, with a cadence of 96 minutes for $6.4\,\mathrm{d}$ between July 13 and July 18, 2016 and a central wavelength of about $\sim 1930\,\AA$ \parencite{mchardy2018x}.\\ 
All continuum light curves follow a broadly similar shape (see Figure \ref{fig:continua_lightcurves}). The light curves start at high flux and then decrease by about $50$--$80\%$ relative to their maxima within the first 1–4 days, reaching a minimum between days 4 and 5. Afterwards, the flux increases again in all light curves. The UV continua show a plateau-like behaviour from days 6 to 9, followed by a smaller decline between days 9 and 13, before rising again to a peak around day 13. A similar pattern is also noticeable in the other continuum light curves, but becomes less clear at higher wavelengths. While the optical continua also show higher flux levels between days 6 and 9 than between days 9 and 13, the NIR continua exhibit a plateau over this interval. Towards the end of the campaign, the flux decreases again towards a minimum, with smaller short-term fluctuations, followed by a slight rise in the final days of the campaign in the UV and NIR bands. \\
The estimation of the physical distance between the SMBH and the region from which the emission lines originate is the main goal of this reverberation mapping analysis. Assuming that the continuum radiation originates from the accretion disk, it is common to use the bluest available continuum as reference for the time lag estimation, which is expected to emerge closest to the SMBH \parencite{peterson1993}. As a photometric light curve, the \textit{Swift} UVW2 light curve covers a broad range of the UV continuum with a high sampling \parencite{mchardy2018x}. Therefore it is used as proxy for the continuum emission in this analysis. \\
Comparing the \textit{Swift} UVW2 light curve to the measured  broad emission line light curves, they exhibit an equally strong decrease in flux, followed by a central part with higher flux and another decrease (see. Figure \ref{fig:emission_line_lightcurves} and \ref{fig:UV_emission_line_lightcurves}). By comparing these features, a correlation of the emission line light curves to the \textit{Swift} UVW2 light curve is already apparent with noticeable time lags which is further investigated in Section \ref{sec:time_lag_bh_mass}.

\subsection{Variability Statistics}
The variability of the emission lines and continua light curves is quantified by adopting the variability statistics definition by \cite{rodriguez1997steps}. They define the maximum-to-minimum flux ratio $R_\mathrm{max}$ and the fractional variability	$F_\mathrm{var}$ as two common measures of variability. $R_\mathrm{max}$ is defined as the ratio of the extreme of the integrated fluxes, $F_\mathrm{min}$ and $F_\mathrm{max}$, and  $F_\mathrm{var}$ as: 
\begin{equation}
	F_\mathrm{var}=\frac{\sqrt{\sigma_F^2-\Delta^2}}{\left<F\right>}.
\end{equation}
Here, $\sigma_F^2$ denotes the standard deviation, $\left<F\right>$ the mean flux and $\Delta^2$ the mean square value of the flux uncertainties $\Delta_i$, which is defined by:
\begin{equation}
	\Delta^2 = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N}\Delta_i^2
\end{equation}\\
The obtained $R_\mathrm{max}$ and $F_\mathrm{var}$ values of the continuum light curves indicates high variability in the UV regime with a decreasing trend towards the NIR regime (see Table \ref{tab:varstatistics_continua}). While the UV continuum around $1150\,\AA$ exhibits the highest values for $R_\mathrm{max} = 2.58$ and $F_\mathrm{var} = 0.28$, they continuously drop towards the most red continua around $8900\,\AA$, which shows values of $R_\mathrm{max} = 1.33$ and $F_\mathrm{var} = 0.08$.\\
The Balmer emission lines exhibit lower variability compared to the continua, which increases towards the higher-order Balmer lines, with values between $R_\mathrm{max} \simeq 1.15-1.52$ and $F_\mathrm{var} \simeq 0.03-0.1$. Together with H$\alpha$ and H$\beta$, the low-ionization O\,\textsc{i}$\,\lambda 8446$ line exhibits the lowest variability of the measured emission lines with $R_\mathrm{max} = 1.22$ and $F_\mathrm{var} = 0.04$. Ly$\alpha$ exhibits comparable variability towards the higher order Balmer lines with $R_\mathrm{max} = 1.42$ and $F_\mathrm{var} = 0.1$, as well as He\,\textsc{i}$\,\lambda5876$ with $R_\mathrm{max} = 1.62$ and $F_\mathrm{var} = 0.09$. The higher ionized He\,\textsc{ii}$\,\lambda\,1640$ line shows a significant higher variability with $R_\mathrm{max} = 3.12$ and $F_\mathrm{var} = 0.31$, while He\,\textsc{ii}$\,\lambda\,4686$ exhibits comparable values to He\,\textsc{i}$\,\lambda\,5876$ with $R_\mathrm{max} = 1.48$ and $F_\mathrm{var} = 0.11$. The high ionization lines tend to higher variability with $R_\mathrm{max}$ between $1.44-1.73$ and $F_\mathrm{var}$ between $0.9 - 0.15$.\\
It shows that the higher ionized emission lines tend to higher variability with the exception of He\,\textsc{ii}$\,\lambda\,4686$ (see Table \ref{tab:varstatistics_lines}). A possible explanation for its lower fractional variability compared to  He\,\textsc{ii}$\,\lambda\,1640$ could be the blend with the Fe\,\textsc{ii} band emission between approximately $ 4489\,\AA$ -- $4629\,\AA$, which adds a non variable component to the flux measurement of the light curve.

\subsection{Uncertainty Estimation}
The uncertainties of the continuum and emission line light curves are estimated based on the spectral noise and a systematic uncertainty introduced by the intercalibration.\\
For the continuum light curves, the per-epoch noise is estimated as 
\begin{equation}
	\sigma_i^{\mathrm{cont}} = \frac{\sigma_{f_i}}{\sqrt{N}}, 
\end{equation} 
where $\sigma_{f_i}$ denotes the standard deviation of the flux density within the selected continuum window in epoch $i$, and $N$ denotes the number of pixels within that window. For the emission line light curves, the noise is estimated from the per-epoch noise in the interpolated pseudo-continuum, $\sigma_{i}^{\mathrm{p.\,cont}}$, as
\begin{equation}
	\sigma_{i}^{\mathrm{line}} = \frac{\sigma_{i}^{\mathrm{p.\,cont}} \, \Delta\lambda}{\sqrt{N}},
\end{equation}
where $\Delta\lambda$ denotes the integration range of the emission line and $N$ is the number of pixels across the integration window.\\
Since each epoch is scaled to the flux of the narrow [O\,\textsc{iii}] $\lambda5007$ line after the intercalibration, the systematic uncertainty introduced by the intercalibration is estimated from its fractional variability $F_{\mathrm{var}}^{\mathrm{[O\,\textsc{iii}]}\,\lambda5007}$:
\begin{equation}
	\sigma_i^{\mathrm{cal.}} = F_{\mathrm{var}}^{\mathrm{[O\,\textsc{iii}]}\,\lambda5007} \, f_i.
\end{equation}
The total uncertainty of the measured flux $f_i$ at epoch $i$ is then given with
\begin{equation}
	\sigma_i^\mathrm{tot,\,cont} = \sqrt{\left(\sigma_i^{\mathrm{cal.}}\right)^2 + \left(\sigma_i^{\mathrm{cont}}\right)^2},
\end{equation}
for the continuum light curves and 
\begin{equation}
	\sigma_i^\mathrm{tot,\,line} = \sqrt{\left(\sigma_i^{\mathrm{cal.}}\right)^2 + \left(\sigma_{i}^{\mathrm{line}}\right)^2},
\end{equation}
for the emission line light curves. 


\section{Time Lag}
\label{sec:time_lag_bh_mass}
To measure the time lag between variation of the continuum emission and the response of the broad emission lines, the emission line light curves are correlated with the photometric \textit{Swift} UVW2 light curve presented in \cite{mchardy2018x}. This is done by determining the cross-correlation function (CCF) and deriving the centroid of the top 20\% of the CCF peak, as described in Section \ref{subsec:rm_ccf}. This calculation is performed with GECHO, which offers the interpolated cross-correlation function (ICCF) method \parencite{gaskell_peterson1986} and the Discrete Correlation Function (DCF) method \parencite{edelson1988discrete} for the CCF. In this work, the ICCF method is applied, where the light curves are normalized by subtracting the respective mean value from each data point and dividing by the standard deviation. To increase the effective sampling and enable the correlation of two unevenly sampled light curves, flux values between data points are linearly interpolated for both light curves. The centroid uncertainties are estimated using the flux randomization/random subset selection (FR/RSS) method described by \cite{Peterson_1998b}, with $10.000$ iterations and a time lag bin size of $0.5$\,days. The normalized \textit{Swift} UVW2 light curve and the emission lines, together with the corresponding CCF and centroid distribution, are displayed in Figures \ref{fig:ccfs_optical} and \ref{fig:ccfs_UV}. In addition, the auto-correlation function of the UVW2 light curve and its CCF with the continuum light curve around $1150\,\AA$ are delivered and displayed in the top and bottom panels of the figures, respectively. \\
Overall, the CCFs of all measured emission lines exhibit strong correlations with peak maximum values between 0.7 and 0.9, with the exception for O\,\textsc{i}\,8446 with approximately $\sim 0.6$. Since the \textit{Swift} UVW2 light curve lags behind the light curve of the $1150\,\AA$ continuum by approximately $\sim 0.5$\,days, this offset is added to the measured emission line time lags to estimate the characteristic BLR radii, which are listed in Table \ref{tab:lags_UVW2}.\\
Comparing the time lags of the Balmer lines, an order dependency becomes apparent. 
The lower-order Balmer lines H$\alpha$ and H$\beta$ exhibit time lags of $3.3 \ensuremath{_{-0.6}^{+1.1}}$ and $2.3 \ensuremath{_{-0.3}^{+1.0}}$ days, respectively, while the higher-ordered lines H$\gamma$ and H$\delta$ show shorter time lags of $1.7 \ensuremath{_{-0.4}^{+0.2}}$ and $1.7 \ensuremath{_{-0.4}^{+0.4}}$ days, respectively. The  Ly$\alpha$ line exhibits a shorter time lag of  $1.0 \ensuremath{_{-0.2}^{+0.4}}$ days than the Balmer lines. This suggests that the Balmer lines emerge from different distances depending on their order, and that emitting region of Ly$\alpha$ is located at a significantly smaller radius than those of the Balmer lines\\
The helium lines He\,\textsc{i}\,$\lambda\,5876$, He\,\textsc{ii}\,$\lambda\,1640$ and He\,\textsc{ii}\,$\lambda\,4686$ show significantly different time lags depending on their ionization level. He\,\textsc{i}\,$\lambda\,5876$ shows a time lag of $3.3 \ensuremath{_{-0.4}^{+0.7}}$ days, consistent with the time lag of H$\alpha$ within uncertainty, while He\,\textsc{ii}\,$\lambda\,1640$ and He\,\textsc{ii}\,$\lambda\,4686$ exhibit significantly shorter lags of $0.3 \ensuremath{_{-0.2}^{+0.6}}\,$days and $0.7 \ensuremath{_{-0.3}^{+0.1}}\,$days, respectively. The high-ionization lines N\,\textsc{v}\,1238, Si\,\textsc{iv}\,1393 and C\,\textsc{iv}\,1548 exhibit consistent time lags of about $\sim 1\,$day, significantly shorter then than the lower ionized Balmer lines and He\,\textsc{i}\,$\lambda\,5876$ line. The difference in time lags between the higher- and lower-ionization lines therefore implies an ionization stratification structure of the BLR, in which higher ionized emission lines originate closer to the SMBH.

\section{Line Profiles}
\label{sec:line_profiles}
The velocity dispersion of the BLR can be parameterized by the line widths of the broad emission line \parencite{peterson2004}. This approach is commonly used in reverberation mapping studies (e.g. \cite{kollatschny1997balmer, denney2006ngc4593}). Therefore, the line profiles of the emission lines are extracted from the mean and rms spectrum. As with the light curve extraction, a subtraction of the underlying continuum is applied to enable comparison between the extracted profiles. Following the same procedure done for the light curves extraction, an underlying linear continuum is interpolated, using line-free wavelength windows on the blue and red side for each emission line in both the mean and rms spectra. The selected boundaries of the pseudo-continua are listed in Table \ref{tab:line_profiles_pseudo}. By subtracting this interpolated continuum, a new zero-flux baseline for each line profile is defined. The flux is normalized to the maximum of each line profile and is subsequently converted to velocity space using the relativistic Doppler equation \parencite{sher1968relativistic} \\ 
\begin{equation}
	\label{eqn:velocity_space}
	v_i = c \cdot \frac{\lambda_i^2 - \lambda_\mathrm{central}^2}{\lambda_i^2 + \lambda_\mathrm{central}^2}.
\end{equation}\\
Here, $\lambda_i$ denotes the wavelength values, $\lambda_\mathrm{central}$ the central wavelength of the emission line in rest-frame and $c$ the speed of light. For the doublets N\,\textsc{v}$\,\lambda\lambda 1283,\,1242 $, Si\,\textsc{iv}$\,\lambda\lambda 1393,\,1402 $ and C\,\textsc{iv}$\,\lambda\lambda 1548,\,1550$, the rest-frame wavelength of the second line is used as the central wavelength of the emission line. An overview of the mean and rms profiles for each emission line is displayed in Figure \ref{fig:line_profiles} and described in the following.

\subsubsection{Balmer lines}
The mean profiles of the Balmer lines share an overall asymmetric shape, characterized by a steeper blue wing and broader red wing. The latter is not visible in the mean profile of H$\gamma$, since its red wing is blended with [O\,\textsc{iii}] $\lambda\,4363$ emission.\\
The rms profiles of the Balmer lines share an overall similar asymmetric double-peaked shape, with the blue peak exhibiting higher flux (see Figure \ref{fig:rms_Balmer}). The two peaks are consistently located at approximately $500\,\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}}$ and $2000\,\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}}$ for all measured Balmer lines, resulting in a shift of the rms profiles towards positive velocities. While the profiles of H$\alpha$ and H$\beta$ show similarly steep slopes in both wings, those of H$\gamma$ and H$\delta$ display more extended blue wings, leading to a close resemblance between the rms profiles of  H$\alpha$ and H$\beta$, and between H$\gamma$ and H$\delta$.


\subsubsection{Ly$\alpha$}

The line profile of Ly$\alpha$ of both spectra exhibits absorption in the blue wing and is blended with N\,\textsc{v}$\,\lambda\lambda 1283,\,1242$ in the red wing. The rms profile is slightly broader than the mean profile, with its peak located at approximately $2000\,\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}}$.

\subsubsection{O\,\textsc{I}$\,\lambda\,8446$}
The mean profile of the O\,\textsc{i}$\,\lambda\,8446$ emission line is blended with the Ca\,\textsc{ii} $\lambda\,8498$, $\lambda\,8542$, $\lambda\,8662$ triplet resulting in an asymmetric double-peak shape. In the rms spectrum, the profile is very noisy and therefore exhibits no pronounced shape. 


\subsubsection{Helium Lines}
The mean profile of He\,\textsc{i}$\,\lambda\,5876$ shows an asymmetric shape with broader red and steeper blue wing. Its rms profile is shifted towards positive velocities, with the peak located at approximately $\sim 1500\,\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}}$ and a line width comparable to that of the mean profile and exhibits an asymmetric shape with a broader blue wing. In contrast to He\,\textsc{i}$\,\lambda\,5876$, the mean profile of the higher ionized  He\,\textsc{ii}$\,\lambda\,1640$ line exhibits a broader blue wing and a steeper red wing, which is blended with the semi-forbidden doublet O\,\textsc{iii}]$\lambda\lambda\,1660,\,1666$ in both the mean and rms spectra. The rms profiles of He\,\textsc{ii}$\,\lambda\,4686$ and He\,\textsc{ii}$\,\lambda\,1640$ show an asymmetric shape with an extended blue wing like seen in the mean profile of He\,\textsc{ii}$\,\lambda\,1640$. The blue wing of the mean profile of He\,\textsc{ii}$\,\lambda\,4686$ is largely blended with Fe\,\textsc{ii} emission.  \\
Comparing the rms profiles of the monitored helium lines, the He\,\textsc{ii} lines exhibit significant broader line widths and no velocity shift relative to their mean profiles in contrast to He\,\textsc{i}$\,\lambda\,5876$ (see Figure \ref{fig:rms_Helium}). 



\subsubsection{N\,\textsc{V}$\,\lambda\lambda 1283,\,1242$}

The N\,\textsc{V}$\,\lambda\lambda 1283,\,1242$ doublet emission is strongly blended with Ly$\alpha$, such that only the red wing of its mean profile is distinguishable from the Ly$\alpha$ mean profile. In addition, the doublet exhibits unresolved variable absorption in the rms spectrum, which further distorts the shape of the rms profile. Comparing the peak locations of both profiles, the rms profile shows no significant velocity shift, like it is seen for the He\,\textsc{ii} lines.

\subsubsection{Si\,\textsc{IV}$\,\lambda\lambda 1393,\,1402$}

The mean profile of the Si\,\textsc{iv}$\,\lambda\lambda 1393,\,1402$ doublet exhibits an asymmetric shape with a more extended blue wing. It is blended with the semi-forbidden doublet O\textsc{iv}]$\,\lambda\lambda\,1397,\,$ $1400$, which cannot be resolved from the line profile. Similar to the mean profile, the rms profile exhibits a very broad blue wing, which additionally includes the variable component of the O\textsc{iv}]$\,\lambda\lambda\,1397,\,1400$ doublet. The rms profile exhibits no significant velocity shift compared to its mean profile.


\subsubsection{C\,\textsc{IV}$\,\lambda\lambda 1548,\,1550$}

The mean profiles of the C\textsc{iv}$\,\lambda\lambda 1548,\,1550$ doublet shows an asymmetric shape comparable to that of  Si\,\textsc{iv}$\,\lambda\lambda 1393,\,1402$. The rms profile shows an even more extended blue wing and a double-peaked structure, caused by absorption near the center of the profile. As seen for the other high-ionization lines, the rms profile shows no significant velocity shift relative to its mean profile.  \\\\
Comparing the mean and rms profiles, a clear distinction between lower- and higher-ionization emission lines is noticeable. The rms profile peaks of the Balmer lines, Ly$\alpha$ and He\,\textsc{i}$\,\lambda5876$ are shifted towards positive velocities relative to the corresponding mean profiles, with most of the rms emission located within the red wing of the mean profile. This indicates that the variability of these lines originates from the receding part of their emitting region. In contrast, the rms profile peaks of He\,\textsc{ii}$\,\lambda1640$ and He\,\textsc{ii}$\,\lambda4686$, as well as those of the high-ionization UV lines N\,\textsc{v}$\,\lambda\lambda 1283,\,1242$, Si\,\textsc{iv}$\,\lambda\lambda 1393,\,1402$ and C\,\textsc{iv}$\,\lambda\lambda 1548,\,1550$ show no velocity shift respective to their mean profiles. Furthermore the higher-ionization emission lines exhibit a blue-weighted rms profile, whereas the lower-ionization lines show a red-weighted rms profile. For N\,\textsc{v}$\,\lambda\lambda 1238,\,1242$, however, this behavior cannot be confirmed due to blending with the Ly$\alpha$ profile.

\subsection{Full Width Half Maximum}
Equally to earlier reverberation mapping studies (e.g. \cite{kollatschny1997balmer, denney2006ngc4593, probst2025emissionlinecontinuumreverberationmapping}), the line width of the line profiles is estimated by their Full Width Half Maximum (FWHM), which is conducted with GECHO. Here, the height of the profiles is defined between the profiles maxima and the zero-flux base line. The mean profile of He\,\textsc{ii}$\,\lambda4686$ is blended with Fe\,\textsc{ii} emission in the blue wing, which makes a measurement of its FWHM not possible. Instead, the FWHM is estimated based on the width of its red wing with $(6900 \pm 1500)\,\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}}$, which is consistent with the order of magnitude of the FWHM of He\,\textsc{ii}$\,\lambda1640$ with $(6000 \pm 400)\,\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}}$  within uncertainties. The same approach is used for N\,\textsc{v}$\,\lambda\lambda 1283,\,1242 $, since it is blended with the Ly$\alpha$ profile in both mean and rms and exhibits not resolved absorption in its blue wing in the rms spectrum.  \\
The FWHM of profiles of O\,\textsc{i}$\,\lambda 8446$ in both spectra are not measured, since the mean profile of the NIR emission line O\,\textsc{i}$\,\lambda 8446$ is blended with the Ca\,\textsc{ii}\,$\lambda8498$, $\lambda8542$, $\lambda8662$ triplet and the rms profile shows high noise and a FWHM measurement was not feasible. The same applies to the profiles of Si\,\textsc{iv}$\,\lambda\lambda 1393,\,1402 $, which are blended with the semi-forbidden line doublet O\textsc{IV}]$\,\lambda\lambda\,1397,\,1400$ such that any FWHM measurement would include contributions from both components and cannot be attributed to Si\,\textsc{iv}$\,\lambda\lambda 1393,\,1402 $ alone. \\
The resulting FWHM values are listed in Table \ref{tab:line_width_FWHM}. The uncertainties are estimated primarily from the instrumental dispersion of the respective gratings (see Table \ref{tab:stis_gratings}), and are adjusted higher according to the noise level of the profiles. For the FWHM of the mean profile of He\,\textsc{ii}$\,\lambda4686$ and both profiles of N\,\textsc{v}$\,\lambda\lambda 1283,\,1242 $, significantly larger uncertainties of $60\%$ rounded to the nearest hundred are adopted, as these values are estimated from only one wing of the profile.\\

The difference of the FWHM values between the higher and lower ionized helium lines as well as the significant higher FWHM of the high-ionization line in respect to the lower ionized Balmer lines therefore implies an ionization dependency of the rms profile line widths.




\section{Black Hole Mass}

The mass of the SMBH is estimated based on the assumption that the velocity of the BLR is virialized and dominated by the gravitational potential of the SMBH \parencite{peterson2004}. Therefore, a virial mass for each measured emission line is determined with the estimated characteristic radii of the BLR and the line widths of the rms profiles, which only cover the variable component of the broad emission line. The virial mass is then adjusted with a scale factor $f$ to account for unknown geometry, kinematic and inclination of the AGN \parencite{peterson2004}. Since the line widths of the rms are estimated with their FWHM, a scale factor of $f=1.8$ is assumed, following \cite{probst2025emissionlinecontinuumreverberationmapping} (see Section \ref{subsec:BHM}). N\,\textsc{v}$\,\lambda\lambda 1238,\,1242$ is excluded in the mass estimation, due to the blend with Ly$\alpha$ and not resolvable absorption in its rms profile, which makes the found FWHM not reliable for the mass estimation. Taking equation \ref{eqn:charc_radius}, \ref{eqn:M_vir} and \ref{eqn:M_BH}, the SMBH mass can be estimated based on the time lag and FWHM for each emission line using: 
\begin{equation}
	\label{eqn:BHM}
	M_{\mathrm{BH}} = 1.8 \cdot \frac{R_{\mathrm{BLR}} \,\mathrm{FWHM}^2}{G}\,.
\end{equation}
The SMBH mass values, as well as the corresponding characteristic radii and FWHM values are listed in Table \ref{tab:BH_mass}.\\
To obtain a final estimation for the SMBH mass, an inverse-variance weighted mean is calculated. Since the uncertainties are asymmetric, the higher value is adopted with $\sigma_i=\mathrm{max}(\sigma_i^-,\sigma_i^+)$.
The weighted mean is then given by
\begin{equation}
	\bar{M}_{\mathrm{BH}}=\frac{\sum_i w_i M_{\mathrm{BH},i}}{\sum_i w_i}\,,\qquad
	w_i=\frac{1}{\sigma_i^2}\,,
\end{equation}
with an uncertainty
\begin{equation}
	\sigma_{\bar{M}}=\left(\sum_i w_i\right)^{-1/2}\,.
\end{equation}
This gives a weighted-mean SMBH mass of $\bar{M}_{\mathrm{BH}}\approx (1.40 \pm 0.12)\times 10^{7}\,M_\odot$.\\

\section{Variation and Bowen Fluorescence of O\,\textsc{I}$\,\lambda 8446$}
\label{sec:BF_Analysis}

Emission-lines in the optical range, such as the Balmer and helium lines, are commonly monitored in reverberation mapping campaigns since they are readily accessible in nearby AGN \parencite{peterson1991steps, kollatschny1997balmer}.  This is not the case for the low-ionization line O\,\textsc{i}$\,\lambda 8446$, which, to this point, has never been monitored in a reverberation mapping analysis. Since it exhibits variability in the HST/STIS dataset (see Table \ref{tab:varstatistics_lines}), it is possible to perform reverberation mapping of this line for the first time.\\ O\,\textsc{i}$\,\lambda 8446$ is also known as a Bowen fluorescence line that can be pumped by Ly$\beta$ emission \parencite{grandi1980}. As described in Section \ref{sec:bowen_fluorescence}, Ly$\beta$ excites O\,\textsc{i} via a near-resonant transition at $\lambda\,1025\,\AA$. This is followed by line transition through the O\,\textsc{i}\,$\lambda\,11287$ transition and subsequently the O\,\textsc{i}\,$\lambda\,8446$ transition, producing the observed O\,\textsc{i}\,$\lambda\,8446$ emission (see Figure \ref{fig:bowen_cascate}). This process has been identified as the dominant excitation mechanism of O\,\textsc{i}\,$\lambda\,8446$ in many AGN, rather than recombination (e.g. \cite{rudy1989detection, rodriguez2002oi, landt2008near}). To investigate if this is the case for NGC\,4593 as well, the line ratio of the quintet line O\,\textsc{i}\,$\lambda\,7774$ and O\,\textsc{i}\,$\lambda\,8446$ is derived. If recombination were a significant driver of the O\,\textsc{i}\,$\lambda\,8446$ emission, the quintet line O\,\textsc{i}\,$\lambda 7774$ would be expected to be comparatively strong, with an expected ratio of $\lambda7774/\lambda\,8446 \approx 1.1\text{--}1.7$ \parencite{grandi1980, landt2008near}. While O\,\textsc{i}\,$\lambda\,7774$ is not detected in the HST/STIS dataset, a ratio of $\lambda7774/\lambda8446 \approx 0.2$ can be estimated, by measuring O\,\textsc{i}\,$\lambda\,7774$ in the MUSE spectrum of NGC\,4593 presented by \cite{Ochmann_2025}. Therefore recombination can be excluded as a significant excitation mechanism for O\,\textsc{i}$\,\lambda\,8446$ in NGC\,4593, which indicated that its variability of O\,\textsc{i}$\,\lambda\,8446$ is primarily driven by L$\beta$ Bowen fluorescence. This allows the Bowen-fluorescence relation of O\,\textsc{i}\,$\lambda 8446$ to be examined in a reverberation mapping analysis for the first time. \\
While Ly$\beta$ is not covered in the spectral range of the HST/STIS dataset, it is possible to use Ly$\alpha$ as a proxy for Ly$\beta$, since it is expected that both lines arise under similar physical conditions. Therefore, the time lags between the \textit{Swift} UVW2 and emission line light curves, as well as the CCFs and time lags between O\,\textsc{i}$\,\lambda\,8446$ and Ly$\alpha$, between H$\alpha$ and Ly$\alpha$, and between O\,\textsc{i}$\,\lambda\,8446$ and H$\alpha$ are derived and shown in Figure \ref{fig:ccfs_Bowen}. All light curves show a strong correlation between $\sim 0.75 - 0.8$, with the exception of the O\,\textsc{i}$\,\lambda 8446$ and \textit{Swift} UVW2 light curves, which show a maximum correlation of only $0.6$. The time lags and their uncertainties are derived as before. O\,\textsc{i}$\,\lambda 8446$ lags behind Ly$\alpha$ by $2.5 \ensuremath{_{-0.3}^{+1.7}}$ days and behind \textit{Swift} UVW2 by $4.7 \ensuremath{_{-1.4}^{+2.4}}$ days, while H$\alpha$ lags behind Ly$\alpha$ by $1.8 \ensuremath{_{-0.5}^{+0.5}}$ days and behind \textit{Swift} UVW2 by $3.3 \ensuremath{_{-0.6}^{+1.1}}$ days. Therefore the direct relation of O\,\textsc{i}$\,\lambda 8446$ to UVW2 shows a slightly higher time lag than H$\alpha$ to UVW2 within uncertainties. Assuming Bowen fluorescence as the dominant excitation mechanism, the lag of O\,\textsc{i}$\,\lambda 8446$ has to be estimated by adding the time lag between the Ly$\alpha$ light curve and the \textit{Swift} UVW2 light curve and the time lag between the Ly$\alpha$ and O\,\textsc{i}$\,\lambda 8446$ light curve. With the Ly$\alpha$ light curve lagging behind the \textit{Swift} UVW2 light curve by $1.0 \ensuremath{_{-0.2}^{+0.4}}$ days, this sums up to a time lag of approximately $\sim 3.5$ days, which is consistent with the time lag between the H$\alpha$ and \textit{Swift} UVW2 light curve within uncertainties. Looking at the O\,\textsc{i}$\,\lambda 8446$ and H$\alpha$ light curves, they show a strong correlation, with O\,\textsc{i}$\,\lambda 8446$ lagging behind H$\alpha$ by about $0.3 \ensuremath{_{-0.1}^{+1.6}}$ days. This implies that O\,\textsc{i}\,$\lambda\,8446$ is emitted at approximately the same distance as H$\alpha$ within uncertainties.
